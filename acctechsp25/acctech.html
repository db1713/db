Designing for Independence: Smart Cane and Mobile App for Abby

Background:
Abby is a college student living with multiple conditions: ADHD, dyslexia, Hashimoto’s disease, and POTS (Postural Orthostatic Tachycardia Syndrome). She frequently experiences fatigue, dizziness, and heart rate spikes that disrupt her daily routine. Her current heart rate trackers are insufficient, as they lack context and overwhelm her with cluttered UIs. Our project goal was to create an access technology system that gives Abby real-time support, personalized insight into her health, and better control over her condition—while keeping cognitive load minimal.

ROLE & DESIGN GOALS

Role

My role involved multiple responsibilities:

  • User Interview: Conducted detailed interviews with Abby to understand her real-world challenges and needs.
  • Concept Development: Conceptualized the smart cane hardware solution tailored to POTS and accessibility needs.
  • UI/UX Design: Designed mid-fidelity screens for both mobile and watch interfaces using Figma.

Design Goals

  1. Understand Triggers: Help users see what caused their heart rate spikes.
  2. Keep it Simple: Use clear, easy-to-read designs for people with ADHD and dyslexia.
  3. Always Be Ready: Let users rely on a smart cane, watch, or phone—whichever they have on hand.
  4. Learn and Suggest: Use past data to offer helpful tips for managing episodes.
  5. Act Fast: Make it easy to get alerts, log data, or ask for help with just a tap.

USER PAIN POINTS

Overwhelming Interfaces

Abby gets easily overwhelmed by cluttered UIs and frequent pop-ups, which make it hard for her to focus or take action quickly.

Lacks Helpful Context

Current heart rate trackers only show raw data and miss important context about what caused the spikes, leaving Abby confused.

Misses Notifications

Visual alerts alone don’t work well for Abby—she often misses them, especially when multitasking or fatigued.

Reading Challenges

Due to dyslexia, Abby struggles with reading and typing in apps that don’t support large text or clean layouts.

WHAT WE DESIGNED

IN-CLASS CRITIQUE & CHANGES MADE

Desire for Live Activity Tracking

Classmates emphasized the importance of real-time tracking of Abby’s current state—whether she’s walking, sitting, running, or sleeping.

Feedback on Modality Shift

We were challenged on requiring users to constantly check their phone. The critique prompted a stronger focus on mobile and watch interfaces instead of relying solely on the phone.

Clarifying Cane Use Experience

The audience asked whether Abby liked the cane. While she didn’t dislike it, we confirmed it was an extra item to carry—thus requiring a design that felt lightweight and discreet.

Problem Statement Refinement

We received critique that our original problem statement lacked focus. We revised it to highlight specific pain points in Abby’s current experience and how our prototype solves them with a clear user flow.

UI Enhancements from Low Fidelity

We upgraded our mobile screens to support more meaningful task execution and added richer context to the home screen, guided by initial secondary research.

Validating the Cane Design

We spoke with Abby to understand her past experience with canes. She appreciated the collapsible version we proposed due to its discreet form and integrated health tracking.

Final Scope Refinement

Based on feedback, we narrowed our problem space and aligned our design more closely with Abby’s personal challenges and expectations.

CANE DESIGN

FEATURE HIGHLIGHTS

Smart Cane

A collapsible mobility aid that’s easy to carry and packed with health tech.

  • Collapsible: Easily fits into a bag when not in use.
  • Heart Rate Sensor: Built into the handle, similar to treadmill grips.
  • Fall Detection: Detects fainting episodes while in use.
  • Haptic Alerts: Vibrates to warn Abby of oncoming episodes.
  • Medicine Storage: Quick-access compartments for emergency meds.

Phone App

iOS-native app that supports Abby with real-time alerts and detailed episode logging.

  • Alerts: Subtle system-style vibration notifications during spikes.
  • Quick Actions: Options to log the episode, dismiss as false positive, or call for help.
  • Support Screen: Helps nearby people assist Abby during an episode.
  • Logging: Records symptoms, activity, mood, medications, and notes.
  • Design Language: Follows iOS conventions for familiarity and comfort.

Personalized Insights

Helps Abby make informed choices using pattern recognition and suggestions.

  • Machine Learning: Learns from Abby’s data to identify patterns.
  • Behavioral Suggestions: Provides tips on avoiding triggers or adjusting routines.
  • Health Trends: Helps Abby reflect on how actions influence symptoms.

USER FEEDBACK

Dual-Purpose Cane

Abby appreciated the collapsible cane not just for mobility, but also for its health-tracking features.

Device Flexibility

She liked having fallback options like the watch and phone in case she forgot one.

Intuitive Design

The overall interface was described as clear, accessible, and non-intrusive.

False Positive Control

She appreciated being able to dismiss false alerts, which helped reduce anxiety.

MY PERSONAL REFLECTION

Applying Sketching & HCI Evaluation Techniques

I learned how to blend HCI principles and sketching in real design work. My initial ideas overlooked accessibility, but redesigning a cane helped me integrate tech across domains into something truly functional.

  • Favorite Task: Smart cane design using variable motor ability insights.
  • Evaluation Skill: Adopted POUR accessibility framework for inclusive analysis.
  • Mindset Shift: I now evaluate everyday objects for inclusivity—UI layouts, ramps, signage, even phone texture and button placement.
  • Curiosity: I'm interested in how these principles apply to virtual worlds, not just physical or digital interfaces.

Inclusive Research & Interviewing

Interviewing Abby was my first time speaking with someone with a disability that causes episodic challenges (POTS). This raised the stakes and shaped my understanding of inclusive design with real-world empathy.

  • Challenge: Being empathetic, using appropriate terminology, and active listening.
  • Growth: Observing and asking follow-up questions across two teams made me more adaptive and sensitive.
  • Perspective: I now understand how diverse life is for people with disabilities, especially invisible ones.
  • Surprise Element: Learned how people creatively 'alt-use' tech beyond its intended design.
  • Future Goal: Spend more time with individuals with disabilities to recognize subtle pain points through observation and shared experience.

The Future of Assistive Tech Research

In the next 10 years, assistive VR will likely evolve toward personalized, sensory-rich experiences for people with diverse disabilities—through adaptive interfaces, inclusive avatars, and more natural interactions.

  • Adaptation: Environments that change based on real-time user needs.
  • Multisensory Experiences: Richer tactile, auditory, and visual feedback for inclusivity.
  • Representation: Emphasis on fair and empowering identity through avatar design and storytelling.

Most Significant Learning Experience

This course helped me unlearn old habits and opened my eyes to the potential of accessible-first design. Sometimes doing things differently is better—not just ethically, but functionally and commercially.

  • Key Insight: Accessible design can lead to smarter, more useful products for everyone.
  • Example: AirPods as medical hearing aids—design with purpose expands reach.
  • Mindset Shift: I no longer see accessibility as a checklist, but as a creative opportunity.
